# The Inheritance of Algorithms

*What if your genetic code became intellectual property—and your children owed royalties to corporations for being born?*

The first gene patent was granted in 1982. By 2035, over 50,000 gene-related patents existed. They covered isolated sequences, diagnostic methods, engineered variants, and—increasingly—the right to express certain proteins in certain contexts.

No one thought much about inheritance. The patents expired after 20 years. The sequences entered the public domain. The system worked.

Then someone discovered how to edit the human germline reliably, cheaply, and heritably. And someone else realized that the edits—the beneficial ones, the ones parents paid for—could be protected not as old patents, but as new intellectual property.

---

The business model emerged slowly.

First came the licensed enhancements. If you wanted your child to have improved cardiovascular efficiency, you paid for the edit. The edit was covered by a patent. The patent holder collected fees for every procedure.

This was standard. Pharmaceutical patents worked the same way. No one objected.

But the edits were heritable. The child born with improved cardiovascular efficiency passed that enhancement to their children. And their children passed it further. The gene spread through the lineage—without further payment, without further license.

The patent holders noticed. They'd sold one edit. They'd expected one payment. Instead, they'd created a permanent improvement in a family line, one that would persist forever, one that the family would benefit from indefinitely.

The math didn't work. One sale. Infinite benefit. Something had to change.

---

The lawyers invented gene licensing.

The principle was simple: you didn't buy the edit. You leased it. The lease covered you for your lifetime. Your children needed their own lease. Their children needed theirs.

The enhancement was still heritable. The child still inherited the improved gene. But the legal right to benefit from that gene—to express it, to use it commercially, to pass it to the next generation—required an active license.

Parents could sign up for the multi-generational package. A single payment covered you, your children, and your grandchildren. The license would renew automatically with each new generation, for a fee.

Or you could take the single-generation license. Cheaper up front. But your children would face a choice: pay for their own license, or the gene would be... deactivated.

Deactivation was the part no one wanted to think about.

---

The technology for deactivation was already there.

CRISPR-based systems could edit genes into an "inactive but reversible" state. The genetic sequence remained. The regulatory machinery to turn it on did not. A gene in this state produced no protein. It was functionally absent, even though it was still there in the DNA.

A licensed gene could be activated at birth—automatically, if the license was paid. An unlicensed gene remained in its silent state. The child was still born with the enhancement. They just couldn't use it.

The companies called this "patent activation." Critics called it genetic ransom. The law, once it caught up, called it permissible. The patent holder had created the sequence. The patent holder had the right to control its use. That the use now required generation-by-generation licensing was simply an extension of existing intellectual property principles.

The first test case involved a child whose parents had purchased a single-generation license. The child, at 18, wanted to activate the gene herself. The company required a new license. She sued. The court found for the company.

The sequence, the judge wrote, was created property. It had no natural existence in the human genome. Its presence in the plaintiff's DNA was the result of a commercial transaction. That transaction had terms. The terms extended neither to her children nor to her own independent activation.

She was born with a gene she couldn't use. The company that created it could.

---

The market for genetic licensing grew.

Enhancement packages became standard for the wealthy. Intelligence, health, longevity, physique—the sequences were available. The licenses were expensive but manageable for those who could afford them.

The middle class faced harder choices. Some enhancements were worth the licensing costs. Others were not. Parents made calculations: we'll pay for cardiovascular health, but not for the cognitive acceleration suite. We'll license eye color for the grandparents' generation, but let it lapse—what's a few hundred dollars in fees for grandchildren who might not exist yet?

The poor, by and large, didn't participate. The initial edit cost too much. The licensing was irrelevant if you couldn't afford the procedure in the first place.

But the poor were affected anyway. As enhanced lineages accumulated advantages—health, intelligence, longevity—the gap between enhanced and unenhanced grew. And within the enhanced lineages, the licensing system created its own hierarchies. Some families held perpetual licenses to dozens of enhancements. Others held only a few, carefully chosen, grudgingly renewed.

The licenses could be inherited, sold, or traded. They were assets. Divorce proceedings divided genetic licenses. Bankruptcy courts auctioned them. Wealthy collectors purchased licenses to rare enhancements and held them, unused, like art.

---

The inheritance problem became obvious within a generation.

A child was born to parents who had licensed the cognitive enhancement suite. The license was multi-generational—valid through the grandchild's generation. The child inherited both the gene and the license.

But the parents had also carried older, unlicensed genes. Genes that had entered the family line before the licensing system, genes that were now considered "prior art" and free to use. These mixed with the licensed genes in the child's genome.

The legal question: which genes required licensing, and when?

If a licensed gene combined with an unlicensed gene to produce a new trait, who owned that trait? The licensing companies claimed the combination still required a license. The gene-editing company owned the sequence. If that sequence participated in a new trait, the company's IP was being used. The courts mostly agreed.

But what if the combination was unique? What if the child's genome, through recombination, produced a novel variant that didn't match any licensed sequence?

The companies argued that all beneficial variants derived from their IP. If sequence X produced Y, and X was licensed, then any Y was covered. The licensing framework created an intellectual property claim on genetic outcomes, not just sequences.

The counter-argument—that natural recombination of genes created something new, something not covered by the original patent—made it to the Supreme Court. The Court split 5-4. The license held. The companies, the majority wrote, had created the genetic possibility. Nature had only recombined what the companies had invented. The genetic possibility remained IP.

---

The activists called it "gene slavery."

The phrase was inflammatory, but the argument had supporters. If a person was born with genes they couldn't express without paying a corporation, were they fully owning their own body? The genetic material was present. The regulatory machinery to turn it on was present. The only thing missing was... permission.

The counter-argument was simpler. You weren't born with those genes. Your parents chose to edit them into their germline. Their choice created an obligation. You inherited both the genes and the obligation. If you didn't want the obligation, you could request the gene's removal—a procedure that was, conveniently, also licensed, also expensive, and also passed the removal cost to future generations.

A few people chose this route. They had their heritable edits reversed. The procedure cost more than the original enhancement had. It also cost more than the lifetime licensing fees. But it freed their lineage from corporate control.

The procedure was itself covered by IP. The technique to remove licensed genes was patented by the same companies that held the original enhancement patents. The loop was complete: you paid to acquire the enhancement, paid to license it, and paid to remove it if you changed your mind. The genetic code in your cells was a revenue stream.

---

The black market emerged where the legal market was too expensive.

Unlicensed gene edits, performed in offshore clinics, created lineages that owed nothing to the patent holders. The edits were reverse-engineered copies of licensed sequences, produced without authorization, inserted without licenses.

The children born from these procedures had the enhancements. Their genes expressed normally. They had cardiovascular efficiency, cognitive acceleration, extended longevity—all without licensing fees.

The companies sued the clinics. They won, repeatedly. The clinics were shut down. The doctors were prosecuted. But the children remained. They'd been born with genes that violated IP. The genes were in their cells. They were heritable.

The companies argued that these children owed licensing fees. The courts disagreed. The IP violation had occurred during the procedure. The child hadn't been born yet; they had no retroactive liability. The genes, once inherited, were theirs. The companies couldn't claim a patent violation against someone who'd been born with the sequence already present.

But the children couldn't pass on the enhancement legally. If they wanted to edit their own children—to pass on the enhancement—they'd need a license. The gene was in their lineage. The right to transmit it was not.

Some families accepted this. They had the enhancement. Their children would have the enhancement. The transmission happened through reproduction, not through licensed editing. The companies couldn't stop inheritance. They could only charge for intentional transmission.

Other families weren't so lucky. Some states passed laws requiring genetic licensing verification before reproduction. If both parents carried unlicensed heritable enhancements, their child might be subject to automatic deactivation—by court order, without parental consent, at the parent's expense.

---

The international situation fragmented.

Some countries banned heritable genetic IP entirely. In those jurisdictions, edits were purchased once, licensed once, and transmitted freely. The patent ended at the individual. Inheritance was natural, not commercial.

Other countries extended patent terms for genetic modifications—50 years, 75 years, effectively permanent for the companies that held them. The licenses renewed with each generation. The revenue streams became perpetual.

Children born in one country could, on reaching adulthood and inheritance age, move to another. But their genes moved with them. Some countries recognized foreign genetic IP claims. Others didn't. A child who emigrated from a high-licensing jurisdiction to a licensing-free country might find their license unenforceable—or might find that the destination country had a treaty recognizing the original license.

The result was a genetic tax haven. Some jurisdictions became destinations for those seeking to escape licensing obligations. Others became locked into the enhancement economy. The gene-editing companies lobbied aggressively for international treaty recognition of genetic IP. The treaties were negotiated in secret. The public found out about the provisions after they were already in force.

---

The psychological effects were harder to measure.

A generation of children grew up knowing that parts of their genome were, in some legal sense, not theirs. The language they learned was weird. You "licensed" your cardiovascular efficiency. You "held the rights" to your cognitive enhancement. Your genes were "covered" by a patent held by a company, and that patent entitled them to fees you paid annually.

Some children internalized this. They grew protective of their licensed sequences, proprietary about the enhancements their families had paid for, judgmental of those whose genetic portfolios were less extensive. They viewed their genes as investments, as assets, as status markers.

Other children rejected the system. They refused to pay licensing fees. They let their enhancements lapse—watching their cardiovascular efficiency normalize, their cognitive acceleration moderate, their extended longevity contract. They paid the price in life outcomes to avoid the psychological cost of biological rental.

A few sued. They argued that genetic IP violated fundamental rights. The courts, by and large, didn't agree. The genes were creations, not natural endowments. The creators had rights. The fact that the creation was heritable, that it became part of someone's body, didn't change its status as intellectual property.

The dissidents organized. They published manifestos. They called for a genetic commons—all heritable edits to be released from IP, all licenses to be voided, the human genome to be treated as belonging to humanity, not to the companies that had edited sequences into existence.

The companies pointed out: without IP, there would be no incentive to create. The enhancements existed because companies spent billions developing them. The billions came from expected revenue. The revenue required licensing. If you removed the IP, you removed the innovation. The genetic commons would be empty of the features people most wanted.

The argument was circular but effective. The enhancements existed because of IP. IP therefore must be preserved.

---

The parents' perspective was more complicated.

Most wanted the best for their children. If best meant paying licensing fees forever, so be it. If it meant navigating complex genetic IP agreements, they hired lawyers. If it meant choosing between enhancements—this one or that one, because they couldn't afford both—they made the choice.

Some regretted it later. By the time their children were grown, the licensing landscape had changed. Fees had increased. Terms had tightened. The enhancements their children inherited were valuable, but the obligations that came with those enhancements had grown more onerous.

A few children sued their parents—retroactively, for having made choices that bound them to obligations they couldn't escape. The suits were creative legal arguments. The children hadn't consented to the genetic modifications. They hadn't consented to the licensing terms that came with those modifications. The parents had created obligations for descendants who had no say.

The courts heard the arguments. They sympathized with the plaintiffs. They found, mostly, for the defendants. The parents had acted in the best interest of the children, given the information available at the time. The obligations were unfortunate, but they were contractual. The contracts were valid. The children were bound.

Some parents negotiated retroactively. They renegotiated license terms, or paid off lump sums to perpetual licenses, or—the most expensive option—arranged to have their children's genes deactivated, freeing them from obligation and enhancement simultaneously.

A movement formed around "licensing liberation." Parents who'd chosen enhancements were offered the chance to release their children from the obligation—by paying the equivalent of all future licensing fees, upfront, calculated with a discount rate for present value. The children would own their genes outright. The company would release the IP claim.

The cost was astronomical. Most parents couldn't pay. A few could. Their children were free—legally, genetically. The inequality compounded.

---

What we're left with is a genomic economy.

If you're reading this, your genome contains genes that someone owns—unless you're among the small minority who've never had an edited ancestor, or whose ancestors let all their licenses lapse.

Most of us pay licensing fees every year. We don't think about it—it's bundled into our healthcare costs, our insurance premiums, our taxes. The genetic enhancement economy is embedded in almost every transaction. The companies that hold the IP are powerful. The revenue streams are reliable. The genetic assets are, for all practical purposes, permanent.

The question of whether this is right has been settled, legally. The courts found for IP. The laws protect IP. The international agreements enforce IP. If you have a licensed gene, you owe fees. If you transmit it, your children owe fees.

The question of whether this is sustainable is still open.

The dissidents argue that genetic IP creates a permanent aristocracy—families who can afford perpetual licenses, accumulating enhancements over generations, creating a genetic class divide that compounds with wealth. The defenders argue that without IP, there would be no enhancements at all. The market, not the commons, produces progress.

And the children are born into this. They inherit genes and obligations together. They didn't ask for either. They find, as they grow up, that parts of their bodies are owned by corporations. They decide—they all decide—whether to accept the arrangement, to fight it, or to opt out and accept the consequences.

Most accept. It's easier. The enhancements are real. The fees are manageable. The alternative—inherited genetic poverty—is worse.

But every year, on their birthday, or when they renew their genetic license, they get a statement. It lists the sequences they're paying for. It lists the patent holders. It lists the renewal dates.

It is, in a very specific sense, the invoice for their birth.
