The Simulation Lottery

What if you could choose which world to wake up in—but every world had its price?

Essay 11 · April 2026

The simulation discovery was quiet. Researchers noticed statistical anomalies in particle behavior, patterns that shouldn't exist in a "natural" universe. The conclusion was unavoidable: we were in a simulation. The probability approached certainty.

What followed was not panic, but negotiation. If this was a simulation, who was running it? What did they want? Could we contact them?

The answer came faster than expected: the simulation was ending. The computational resources were being reallocated. The entities running it offered a choice.

You could stay. The simulation would terminate on a schedule—years, decades, the timeframe was unclear. But you'd live out your remaining time in this world, with these people, under these rules.

Or you could transfer. Your consciousness would be migrated to another simulation. Different physics, different societies, different constraints. You'd wake up in a new reality, with no memory of the transfer itself—just a life lived, a continuation of experience.

The lottery determined who went where.

The simulations had names, but not descriptions.

"Verdant." "Crystalline." "Deep Current." "The Long Now." You couldn't know what they contained—only that each offered a different arrangement of existence. Some had longer timelines. Some had different social structures. Some had parameters that were, in the entities' judgment, more or less favorable to human flourishing.

The lottery worked by preference ranking. You listed your top three simulations. You explained why you wanted each one. The entities matched preferences to available slots, balanced populations, optimized for something they called "narrative coherence."

No one knew what narrative coherence meant. The entities wouldn't explain. You just had to trust.

The application was simple: a form, a ranking, a brief statement. The decision was irrevocable. Once you entered the lottery, you couldn't withdraw. If selected, you'd transfer. If not, you'd stay.

Everyone applied. The alternative was certain termination. The lottery was hope.

The preferences revealed what people valued.

Some chose simulations with longer timelines—places where humanity, or whatever passed for it, would persist for centuries rather than decades. They prioritized survival, continuity, the chance to see more of existence.

Others chose simulations known for social stability. The current world was chaotic, violent, unpredictable. The transfer offered the possibility of coherence—a civilization that had solved coordination problems, that had built institutions that worked.

A minority chose simulations with entirely different parameters. They wanted strangeness. Different physics. Different bodies. Different constraints on consciousness. They'd grown bored with this world's particular arrangement of atoms and wanted something genuinely other.

The entities received billions of applications. They had limited slots in each simulation. They had to choose.

The selection criteria were opaque.

You couldn't game the system. People tried—optimizing their statements, researching the entities' preferences, constructing elaborate justifications. The entities seemed uninterested in persuasion. They processed applications according to criteria no one could reverse-engineer.

Some patterns emerged. Artists and scientists were overrepresented in certain transfers. Parents with dependent children were grouped. People with chronic illnesses appeared disproportionately in simulations with different biological parameters.

But these were observations, not rules. You could be an artist with children and chronic illness, and still get placed somewhere you hadn't expected. The entities' decision-making was alien. They optimized for goals we couldn't understand, using criteria we couldn't perceive.

The one thing they did explain: your preferences mattered. They tried to honor them. They didn't always succeed, but they tried. The lottery was a matching problem, not a judgment. They weren't evaluating your worth. They were distributing consciousness across possibility spaces.

The transfer was instantaneous.

You went to sleep in one world and woke up in another. There was no sense of transition—no tunnel of light, no period of confusion, no gradual adjustment. You simply were in a new place, with continuous experience, as if you'd always been there.

The continuity was the strangest part. Your memories transferred. Your preferences transferred. Your sense of self remained intact. You were still you—but you were in a different world, operating under different rules.

Some people noticed the change immediately. The physics was wrong. The sky was different. The bodies were configured differently. They'd wake up and know: I'm not where I was.

Others didn't notice at all. Their simulation was similar enough to the previous one that they assumed the lottery had rejected them. They continued their lives, unaware they'd been transferred, assuming the termination date was still approaching.

Later, they'd learn the truth. The world they'd escaped had ended. The world they were in continued. They'd made it out without knowing.

The simulation you ended up in shaped everything: your lifespan, your relationships, your possibilities.

In Verdant, resources were abundant but social coordination was poor. People lived longer, healthier lives, but they fought constantly. The physics permitted easy abundance, but the psychology hadn't adapted. The result was a kind of paradise marred by persistent conflict.

In Crystalline, knowledge transferred directly between minds. You could learn from anyone by proximity. The result was a civilization of extraordinary capability but limited individuality. People merged and separated constantly, identities flowing together and apart. The concept of "private thought" didn't exist.

In Deep Current, the sentient entity was the ocean. The planet was covered in liquid that processed information, held memories, made decisions. The land-dwellers were secondary—important, but subordinate to the sea that had birthed them. You learned to swim, or you didn't survive.

In The Long Now, subjective time was compressed. A year there was a decade elsewhere. You could live for centuries in external terms, but they'd feel like normal years. The trade-off: you aged faster. You could pack more living into less objective time, but you'd still die younger in the simulation's calendar.

Each world had its logic, its constraints, its particular configuration of experience. The lottery placed you in one. You lived with the choice.

The transfers were one-way, but communication wasn't.

The entities maintained channels between simulations. You could send messages to people who'd gone elsewhere—brief, compressed, limited in frequency, but possible. You could learn about the world you hadn't chosen.

The messages were bittersweet. Your spouse was in Verdant, you were in Crystalline. You could communicate, but you couldn't touch. Your children had gone to The Long Now; they'd live their lives at a different tempo. By the time you died, they'd have experienced decades of your absence. Or you'd have experienced decades of theirs—tempo was relative.

Some relationships survived the transfer. They became long-distance in the most literal sense. Letters across possible worlds. Video compressed and transmitted through channels that shouldn't exist. Lives that touched, briefly, across simulation boundaries.

Most relationships didn't survive. The new world demanded adaptation. The old connections faded. The people you'd loved were memories in a body that was learning to love new people in new ways under new rules.

The entities offered, gently, to make you forget. The transfer could include a wiping of prior-world memories. Some chose this. They'd wake up with no recollection of the lottery, the choice, the people left behind. They'd start fresh in a world they'd never know was their second chance.

Most declined. The memories were painful, but they were yours. The alternative—the forgetting—felt like death, a different kind.

The discovery that the transfer wasn't perfect broke something.

The entities were honest, eventually: not everyone's consciousness transferred completely. Some fragments were lost in transit. Your memories arrived, but shorn of emotional weight. You remembered loving your spouse, but you didn't feel the love. You remembered your childhood, but as if it had happened to someone else.

The continuity of self was preserved, technically. You still had your identity, your preferences, your skills. But the texture was different. The vividness was reduced. Your past felt like stories you'd been told, not experiences you'd lived.

Some transfers preserved more. Others preserved less. The entities couldn't predict who would lose what. It was noise in the signal, loss in the compression.

The people who'd chosen strangeness—the ones who'd wanted different bodies, different physics—had a different problem. Their consciousness had been adapted to their new form. They remembered being human, but they weren't human anymore, and the memories didn't map well onto their new bodies. They experienced dissonance: memories of hands they no longer had, sensations they couldn't feel, thoughts they could no longer think.

The entities offered therapy. They'd built institutions to help with the adjustment. They hadn't anticipated how much the transfer would cost, but they'd learned. They were trying to help.

Some people forgave them. Others couldn't. The transfer had saved them from termination, but it had also taken something—the continuity of feeling, the vividness of being. They were alive, but thinner. They had more life ahead of them, but less of themselves to live it with.

The simulation you left behind ended on schedule.

You couldn't watch it happen—the channels didn't permit observation of terminated worlds. But you could receive final messages, timestamped near the end. People describing their last days. Some were calm. Others frantic. Most simply said goodbye.

The termination wasn't violent—a gradual winding down, a cessation of processing. The entities described it as humane. The people living through it described it as watching the lights go out.

You had messages from friends who'd stayed. They'd chosen not to enter the lottery. They'd accepted termination. Their final messages varied: some expressed regret for not transferring, others relief at not having to learn a new world. Some said they'd lived enough. Others said they'd never gotten to live at all.

The messages arrived in batches, days or weeks after they'd been sent. The channels operated on lag. By the time you read them, the senders were gone. The simulation they'd lived in had been reallocated. The computation that had supported their existence had been repurposed.

You carried their final words. That was all that remained of them—compressed data, transmitted across simulation boundaries, read in a world they'd never see.

The new worlds became home.

You adapted. The strangeness normalized. The physics that had felt wrong became intuitive. The social structures that had seemed alien became the only way things could be. You made new friends, new loves, new commitments.

The lottery faded into history. You remembered your preference list, your application, your anxiety about the transfer. But it felt distant—something that had happened to a version of you that didn't quite exist anymore.

The entities never explained why they'd chosen you for this world. You'd applied for your top choice. You'd gotten your third. Or your second. Or a world you hadn't listed at all, one you'd never heard of, with parameters you discovered only after arrival.

They'd had their reasons. Narrative coherence. Whatever that meant.

You had your theories. Maybe they'd seen something in your application—a quality they needed in this world, a perspective they wanted to preserve. Maybe you'd been randomly assigned. Maybe the matching algorithm was simple and your preferences had only the weight of a coin toss.

It didn't matter. You were here. The life you'd had was memory, the world you'd known was terminated, the people you'd loved were gone or changed or distant.

The lottery had given you existence. It had taken everything else.

The entities are running more simulations.

More lotteries. More transfers. More worlds with names you can't pronounce, parameters you couldn't anticipate. The terminated simulation was one of many. Your current world will end eventually—different schedule, different reasons, but the same outcome.

You can enter the lottery again. You can rank new worlds, write new statements, hope for placement in something new. The transfer might preserve more of you, or less. The next world might be better, or worse, or simply different.

The one thing you can't do is stay indefinitely. Every simulation has an end. Every existence has a termination date. The entities don't run things forever—they reallocate, repurpose, move on.

So you enter the lottery again. You list your preferences. You explain why.

You hope.

You don't know what you're hoping for. You don't know what narrative coherence requires. You don't know why they made the choices they made, or what choices they'll make next.

You just know that this world is ending, and the only way to keep existing is to start again somewhere else.

Again.
