Designed Minds

What if parents could genetically optimize their children?

Essay 2 · April 2026

The clinic was in a glass tower in Singapore, though it could have been anywhere. Every major city had one by now. You walked in with a partner, a dream, and a bank account. You walked out with something more precise than hope.

They called it "optimization." The brochure used words like "potential" and "wellness" and "long-term outcomes." The geneticists spoke in percentages—risk reduction, trait amplification, likelihood thresholds. No one used the word "design." Design implied a plan. What happened here was more like pruning.

The first generation came out fine. Better than fine.

The Enhanced, as they were called, were taller, healthier, sharper. They got sick less often. They learned faster. They were, by every metric their parents had paid for, superior.

The parents were proud. The grandparents were ambivalent. The uncles and aunts—those who hadn't been able to afford the procedure—watched their own children with new eyes, noticing every ordinary flaw.

The gap between Enhanced and unmodified wasn't dramatic at first. Just a gradient. The Enhanced were at the top of the distribution instead of the middle. But when the baseline shifts, everything shifts. A few percentage points of cognitive advantage, spread across an entire life, became a different kind of adulthood.

By thirty, the Enhanced had accumulated degrees, networks, opportunities. The unmodified had... done fine. Not badly. Just normally.

And "normally" was starting to feel like a verdict.

The ethical debates were furious and abstract.

Philosophers argued about autonomy—the child hadn't consented to the modification, after all. But the child also hadn't consented to their genetic default, if "default" was even a meaningful word. Was a randomized embryo more ethical than an optimized one?

The religious objections were clearer: playing God, interfering with nature's plan. Except nature's plan included Tay-Sachs, Huntington's, and a thousand other cruelties. No one objected when parents screened for those.

The more subtle complaints took longer to articulate. The Enhanced were... fine. They were healthy, capable, well-adjusted. And that was strange. They were uniformly fine. The distribution curve had been flattened at the top. The outliers were rarer now. The eccentric, the obsessive, the inexplicably brilliant—they'd been optimized into competence.

This was the paradox no one had predicted: the elimination of the bottom had also, gradually, eliminated the strange peaks. When you prune toward the median of success, you prune away the outliers. The price of optimization was the death of the interesting.

Identity was where it got weird.

The Enhanced knew they were Enhanced. Their parents had told them, usually around adolescence, sometimes earlier. It was supposed to be something to be proud of. An investment, a gift, a leg up.

But knowing you were designed to be good at things changed your relationship to your own competence. Every achievement carried a footnote: of course I'm good at this. I was made to be.

The unmodified children of wealthy parents had a mirror problem. They'd grown up with every advantage—tutors, travel, connections—but they'd never been optimized. They were the control group in a experiment their peers had paid to skip. They'd inherited every expectation with none of the guarantee. They were expected to succeed, but on unmodified hardware.

The Enhanced, meanwhile, had a different anxiety. What if they didn't succeed? They'd been given the best possible start. Failure wasn't impossible—it was just harder to excuse. If an Enhanced child struggled, whose fault was it? Not the genes. Those were checked.

Some Enhanced began to wonder: was their ambition theirs? Or was it another trait, one their parents had selected? They couldn't tell the difference anymore. They wanted things, and achieved things, and couldn't know if the wanting was authentic or programmed.

The therapists saw it first.

A new kind of patient: the Enhancement-weary. Young adults who felt strange in their own competence. They described it like a suit that fit too well—no gaps, no pressure points, but also no air. They were exactly who they were supposed to be, and they didn't know how to find who they might have been.

Some tried to fail on purpose. Dropped out, disappeared, took up obscure hobbies their parents had never imagined. The rebellions were gentle, mostly. The Enhanced were too optimized for self-destruction. Their bodies and minds resisted.

A few went further. Sought out gene therapies that would "undo" their modifications. It was experimental, expensive, and mostly didn't work. You couldn't unsculpt what had been sculpted.

But the impulse itself was information. The Enhanced might not have been designed to feel this dissatisfaction, but they felt it anyway. And that suggested something: the optimization had missed something. Some core self, some ineffable center, remained unmodified. The question was whether that center was something worth preserving, or an artifact of a transition that would, in time, be smoothed away.

The next generation was different.

The early adopters had been conservative, relatively speaking. They'd selected for health, intelligence, emotional stability. The low-hanging fruit. But the market doesn't stay still.

Now parents could select for traits that weren't obviously health-related. Musicality. Photographic memory. Introversion or extroversion. Risk tolerance. Paternal certainty had increased. "Agreeableness" was up 30% in the under-ten cohort.

The children of these selections were strange. They were, by their genetic definitions, successful. But the definition of success had narrowed. The parents had bought their own preferences into their children's DNA.

And this is where the philosophers re-entered, with a new question: was this a kind of cloning? Not literal cloning—you weren't copying a specific genome. But you were copying a specific ideal. If your daughter's personality matched your ideal personality, was she still a separate person? Or was she a kind of auto-biography, written in the language of choice?

The term "designed" came back, with a new weight. These weren't just optimized. They were composed. And composition implied an author.

The long-term question—the one no answer satisfied—was about variance.

Evolution works because it doesn't optimize. It wanders. Tries things. Most fail. But the ones that work, work in unpredictable ways. The genius and the fool and the obsessive and the saint are all part of the same process. You can't have one without the possibility of the others.

The Enhancers had made a bet: that they could cut the failures while keeping the successes. That they could prune the distribution at the bottom without touching the top.

But the data was coming in, and it wasn't clear they'd won. The Enhanced were competent. They were healthy. They were, in aggregate, successful by every metric their parents had optimized for.

But they weren't generating the same kind of outliers. The unmodified cohorts—those who could still afford children, and who had chosen not to—were still producing the eccentric, the obsessed, the inexplicably brilliant. The tails of the unmodified distribution were longer.

This was the trade-off no brochure mentioned. When you optimize for the median, you narrow the distribution. The result was a world of competence, without many peaks.

Whether that was a bug or a feature depended on whether you thought the peaks were worth the valleys.

I met an Enhanced man in a coffee shop in Portland. He was thirty-two, a software architect, conventionally attractive. He'd been modified for mathematical aptitude. His parents had paid extra for "emotional stability," which he described as "a low baseline for despair, but also a low ceiling for joy."

He was thinking about having children. His partner was Enhanced too—different package, similar outcomes. They were both, by any available metric, near the top of their cohort.

"I don't know if I should do it," he said. "The procedure, I mean. For the kid."

I asked him why not.

"Because I can't tell anymore," he said, "which parts of me are me. And I don't know if that's a problem that can be solved. I'm happy. I'm successful. I like my life. But I have no idea who I'd have been without the intervention. And that absence—the not-knowing—feels real. It feels like something was taken."

He stirred his coffee. His hand was steady. Enhanced motor control.

"Maybe the next generation doesn't need to wonder," he said. "Maybe they just get to be the best version of whatever their parents wanted. And maybe that's fine."

He shrugged.

"But I think I want my kid to have the chance to be worse. Just—just to have the chance. To discover it instead of arriving. To earn something, even if it's small. Even if it doesn't matter."

He smiled.

"My parents paid for optimal. I'm thinking about paying for... less."

The future has two branches.

In one, optimization continues. The Enhancers get better at their craft. The distributions narrow. Humanity converges toward a plateau of competent, healthy, reasonably happy people. The outliers fade. The strange and the beautiful and the inexplicably brilliant become rarer, then disappear. The world works. It just stops surprising itself.

In the other, something else happens. A rejection, a fashion shift, a cultural re-evaluation. The Enhanced become a cohort, but not a majority. The unmodified persist, and keep producing variance. The two groups diverge—not in kind, but in shape. One group runs faster. The other wanders further.

The question, from here, isn't which future will happen. It's which future we want. And that question, unlike the genetic ones, can't be answered by looking at a chart.

It has to be answered by asking: what is a person for? To be the best version of themselves? Or to find out what "themselves" might turn out to be?

The answer to that question isn't in any genome. It's in the choice itself. The choice to choose, or to leave unchosen.

The Enhanced made their parents' choices visible. Now their children would have to make their own.
